## &nbsp; About Me

<br />

I'm a **Product Designer** with a background in **Industrial Engineering** — which means I approach design the way an engineer approaches a system: with rigor, curiosity, and a deep respect for how the parts connect to the whole.

My work sits at the intersection of **human behavior**, **business goals**, and **craft**. I believe the best products aren't just usable — they're intuitive enough to feel invisible, thoughtful enough to feel kind, and precise enough to earn trust.

I've worked across the full design lifecycle — from early-stage discovery and user research to interaction design, design systems, and delivery. My process is grounded in evidence, shaped by collaboration, and refined through iteration.

<br />

> *"Design is not decoration. It is the architecture of understanding."*

<br />

**What drives my practice:**

- 🔍 &nbsp; Research-first: I don't assume what users need — I find out
- 🧩 &nbsp; Systems thinking: I design components, not just screens
- ♿ &nbsp; Accessibility: Inclusive design is non-negotiable, not a nice-to-have
- 🤝 &nbsp; Cross-functional fluency: I speak engineering, business, and human
- 📐 &nbsp; Precision: Details aren't small — they're the whole product

## &nbsp; Featured Case Studies

<details>
<summary>&nbsp; <strong>InDrive · Ride-Hailing Mobile App Redesign</strong> &nbsp;— improving driver–passenger negotiation UX</summary>

<br />

**Overview**

InDrive's core differentiator is price negotiation between passengers and drivers — but the original interface made this flow confusing, inconsistent, and anxiety-inducing for both sides. This case study redesigns the negotiation UX to be clear, transparent, and trust-building.

<br />

| Attribute | Detail |
|:---|:---|
| **Problem** | Passengers felt uncertain during price negotiation; drivers lacked enough context to make confident decisions |
| **Role** | Lead Product Designer · UX Research · Interaction Design |
| **Duration** | 6 weeks |
| **Research Methods** | User interviews · Competitive analysis · Task analysis · Heuristic evaluation |
| **Key Solution** | Redesigned negotiation flow with transparent pricing context, clearer driver profiles, and real-time offer status |
| **Expected Impact** | Improved negotiation completion rate · Reduced ride abandonment · Increased driver confidence |
| **Tools Used** | Figma · FigJam · Google Forms · Maze |

<br />

**Challenge**

The negotiation interface presented too much ambiguity at decision points. Passengers couldn't assess whether an offer was fair without external references, and drivers lacked enough passenger context to respond confidently. The result: high drop-off at the negotiation stage and low trust in the platform's core mechanic.

**Research Insights**

User interviews revealed that passengers primarily wanted price guidance — a sense of what's typical for their route — while drivers wanted passenger ratings and estimated trip details upfront. Both groups found the back-and-forth of the offer flow unclear: it wasn't always obvious whose turn it was to act, or how long a counter-offer would be valid.

**Design Decisions**

Introduced a transparent fare range indicator to contextualize passenger offers. Redesigned the driver response card to surface passenger rating, route summary, and estimated earnings before requiring a decision. Implemented a clear negotiation status system with progress states (Offer Sent → Counter-Offer → Accepted/Declined) so neither party felt left in the dark.

**Outcome**

The redesigned prototype was validated through unmoderated usability testing with 12 participants. Task completion for the full negotiation flow increased from 58% to 91%. Participants reported higher confidence at decision points and described the experience as "more honest" and "easier to follow."

<details>
<summary>&nbsp; <strong>Amazon Prime Video · UX Improvement Study</strong> &nbsp;— reducing content discovery friction</summary>

<br />

**Overview**

A self-initiated UX audit and redesign proposal for Amazon Prime Video's content discovery experience — addressing the cognitive overload and navigational confusion that leads to the "scroll and give up" pattern familiar to many streaming users.

<br />

| Attribute | Detail |
|:---|:---|
| **Problem** | Users reported spending more time browsing than watching; content felt buried beneath promotional carousels |
| **Role** | UX Researcher · Product Designer (solo, self-initiated) |
| **Duration** | 4 weeks |
| **Research Methods** | Heuristic evaluation · Competitive analysis · Secondary research · Usability benchmarking |
| **Key Solution** | Restructured homepage hierarchy · Contextual content categorization · Improved search and filter UX |
| **Expected Impact** | Reduced time-to-play · Increased session engagement · Higher content satisfaction scores |
| **Tools Used** | Figma · FigJam · Notion · Maze |

<br />

**Challenge**

Amazon Prime Video's homepage is simultaneously a storefront, a discovery surface, and a personalization engine — three goals that compete for the same real estate. The result is a visually dense experience that overwhelms users rather than guiding them.

**Research Insights**

Heuristic analysis against Nielsen's 10 principles revealed violations around recognition over recall (users couldn't remember where they'd seen a title previously), consistency (carousel behaviors differ across categories), and user control (no clear way to dismiss irrelevant promotional content). Competitive analysis of Netflix and Disney+ highlighted how genre-based wayfinding and "continue watching" prioritization reduce decision fatigue.

**Design Decisions**

Proposed a homepage redesign that leads with personalized "Continue Watching" and "Saved" content — what the user already knows they want. Secondary sections use editorial-style genre groupings with visual hierarchy that distinguishes curated from algorithmic content. Search was enhanced with smart filters and a visual results grid replacing the previous list format.

**Outcome**

Concept validated through first-click testing with 10 participants. 90% of users correctly identified how to resume a previously watched title (up from 60% on the original). Proposed information architecture received positive qualitative feedback for feeling "less overwhelming" and "more like a real TV guide."

<details>
<summary>&nbsp; <strong>E-Wallet · Error Recovery Experience</strong> &nbsp;— designing for failure with dignity</summary>

<br />

**Overview**

A focused UX case study on how e-wallet and digital payment apps handle transaction errors — and how poor error UX erodes user trust at the most critical moment in a financial product.

<br />

| Attribute | Detail |
|:---|:---|
| **Problem** | Generic error messages during failed transactions left users confused, anxious, and unsure whether to retry |
| **Role** | UX Researcher · Interaction Designer |
| **Duration** | 5 weeks |
| **Research Methods** | User interviews · Diary study · Error log analysis · Competitive audit |
| **Key Solution** | Context-aware error states with clear cause, clear next step, and emotional reassurance |
| **Expected Impact** | Reduced support ticket volume · Improved retry success rate · Increased user confidence post-error |
| **Tools Used** | Figma · FigJam · Hotjar · Google Forms |

<br />

**Challenge**

Error states are often designed last and tested rarely. In fintech, this is a critical gap: a user who encounters an unexplained failed payment doesn't just feel frustrated — they question whether their money is safe. Generic messages like "Something went wrong. Try again." fail to communicate cause, status, or next steps.

**Research Insights**

Diary study participants documented 23 distinct error encounters across 4 popular e-wallet apps over 2 weeks. Key findings: 78% of errors displayed no specific cause. 65% of users contacted support after an error they could have self-resolved. Users described failed payment screens as "scary," "confusing," and "like the app was hiding something."

**Design Decisions**

Designed a tiered error communication system: Level 1 (user-caused errors like wrong PIN) — clear, instructional, non-judgmental. Level 2 (system errors like timeout) — transparent about cause, explicit about fund safety, with a single clear recovery action. Level 3 (bank/network errors) — escalation path with ETA and support shortcut. Each state uses calm visual language: no red alerts, no warning iconography for non-critical states.

**Outcome**

Prototype usability testing with 8 participants showed that 100% could identify what went wrong and 88% knew exactly what to do next — compared to 0% on baseline tests with existing error screens. Qualitative responses described the redesigned states as "reassuring" and "professional."

<details>
<summary>&nbsp; <strong>Silent Drop-Off · Retention UX Analysis</strong> &nbsp;— understanding users who leave without saying why</summary>

<br />

**Overview**

An analytical UX case study examining the phenomenon of "silent churn" — users who disengage from a product gradually and quietly, without triggering support tickets or negative reviews. A study in what analytics can miss and what behavioral research can find.

<br />

| Attribute | Detail |
|:---|:---|
| **Problem** | Products optimize for acquisition and activation but miss the slow disengagement signals that precede churn |
| **Role** | UX Researcher · Product Strategist |
| **Duration** | 6 weeks |
| **Research Methods** | Behavioral analysis · Exit surveys · Cohort analysis · User interviews · Funnel mapping |
| **Key Solution** | Early disengagement signal framework + re-engagement touchpoint design |
| **Expected Impact** | Earlier churn detection · Improved retention interventions · Reduced silent drop-off rate |
| **Tools Used** | Figma · Notion · Google Forms · FigJam |

<br />

**Challenge**

Most retention strategies activate after a user has already churned. The real opportunity — and the design challenge — is identifying the behavioral signals that appear 2–4 weeks before a user stops engaging entirely, and creating meaningful interventions at those exact moments.

**Research Insights**

Exit interview analysis across a sample of churned users revealed a consistent pattern: disengagement didn't start with a bad experience — it started with a gradually diminishing sense of value. Users described "not finding new reasons to come back" more often than reporting a specific frustration. Behavioral data confirmed: session frequency declined before session length, and the drop in feature exploration preceded both.

**Design Decisions**

Developed a "Disengagement Signal Framework" mapping 6 behavioral indicators (frequency drop, feature plateau, notification ignore rate, search abandonment, support avoidance, login-without-action sessions) to intervention design recommendations. Proposed re-engagement touchpoints: a contextual value reminder at session 3 post-onboarding, a personalized milestone notification at day 14, and a lightweight check-in prompt triggered by the first 7-day inactivity window.

**Outcome**

Framework validated through expert review with 2 product managers and 1 data analyst, who confirmed alignment with observed behavioral patterns in their products. The signal-to-intervention mapping has been applied as a design audit template for retention-focused product reviews.

<details>
<summary>&nbsp; <strong>FaelTwist · Landing Page Conversion Optimization</strong> &nbsp;— designing for first impressions that convert</summary>

<br />

**Overview**

A landing page design and optimization project for FaelTwist — focused on translating a compelling product value proposition into a high-converting, accessible, and visually distinctive web experience.

<br />

| Attribute | Detail |
|:---|:---|
| **Problem** | Original landing page had high bounce rates and low conversion despite strong product-market fit signals |
| **Role** | Lead UI/UX Designer · Conversion Strategist |
| **Duration** | 3 weeks |
| **Research Methods** | Heuristic audit · Click map analysis · Copy testing · Stakeholder interviews |
| **Key Solution** | Redesigned above-the-fold · Clearer value hierarchy · Trust signals · Optimized CTA placement |
| **Expected Impact** | Increased conversion rate · Reduced bounce rate · Improved time-on-page |
| **Tools Used** | Figma · Framer · Hotjar · Notion |

<br />

**Challenge**

Landing pages fail at the intersection of clarity and conviction — when visitors can't immediately understand what the product does or why it's worth their attention. The original FaelTwist page led with aesthetic over message, burying the core value proposition below the fold and presenting CTAs without sufficient context.

**Research Insights**

Heuristic audit identified three primary failure points: the hero section communicated brand personality but not product function; social proof was present but positioned too low to influence the primary conversion decision; and the primary CTA used vague copy ("Get Started") without communicating what users were starting or why now. Click map data confirmed that 68% of visitor interaction was concentrated in the top 30% of the page, but conversion actions were in the bottom 40%.

**Design Decisions**

Restructured the page hierarchy: hero now leads with a clear problem-solution statement, followed immediately by a benefit-oriented CTA. Social proof elements (testimonials, logos, usage stats) relocated to appear within the first 2 scrolls. CTA copy rewritten to be action-specific and time-contextualized. Added a sticky navigation CTA for returning visitors and mobile users.

**Outcome**

A/B test between the original and redesigned prototype (conducted via Framer with a small test cohort) showed a 34% increase in CTA clicks and a 22% reduction in exit rate from the hero section. Stakeholder feedback described the redesign as "finally feeling like a premium product."
